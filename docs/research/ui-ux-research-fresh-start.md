# Mentor IB Fresh-Start UI/UX Research

**Date:** 2026-04-07
**Status:** Research and recommendation brief
**Scope:** Product positioning, UX strategy, visual direction, design-system reset

## 1. Executive Summary

Mentor IB should adopt a matching-first design language that feels specific, memorable, and IB-native.

The product should avoid a generic tutoring-marketplace aesthetic with polished but interchangeable SaaS styling. The design direction should not be defined by:

- `Airbnb-inspired`
- `search-first`
- `Geist Sans`
- `one brand color + neutrals`
- card-grid browsing as the dominant discovery model

That approach is coherent, but it is too close to the default visual and interaction vocabulary used by many AI projects, marketplaces, and startup templates. It also frames the product as a broad tutor directory when the stronger opportunity is a guided matching tool for IB students in moments of pressure.

## 2. What The Product Must Signal

The experience should not primarily signal:

- browse a catalog
- compare cards
- pick a tutor
- book a lesson

It should strongly signal:

- we understand the IB journey
- we can quickly guide you to the right kind of tutor
- we know the difference between IA rescue, TOK writing, IO rehearsal, exam prep, and weekly support
- we reduce choice anxiety
- we help you decide, not just browse

### Key strategic observations

- The UI is visually tidy, but the overall feel is template-like rather than branded or memorable.
- The search experience uses familiar marketplace patterns: horizontal filters, card grids, pricing, heart/save, and generic list browsing.
- Tutor cards are image-led but not insight-led. They show attributes, but not enough fit rationale.
- The profile page is usable but reads like a database record more than a coached decision page.
- The homepage promise is actually closer to the right direction than the rest of the product: it mentions IA feedback, TOK writing, oral practice, and exam rescue. That language should become the core model, not just hero copy.
- Visual rhythm and brand consistency need to feel deliberate enough to create trust and memorability.
- Several tutor photos look synthetic or inconsistent in style, which hurts trust and reinforces the “AI-built” feeling.

## 3. Competitor Research

I reviewed Mentor IB's intended product category alongside several tutoring platforms:

- Preply
- Wyzant
- GoStudent
- Superprof
- italki

### 3.1 Preply

What works:

- Strong tutor-listing density: rating, reviews, price, student count, lessons taught, languages, and CTA all appear early.
- Search results are list-based, which gives each tutor enough room to explain fit.
- Trial lesson and message actions reduce commitment anxiety.
- The platform is excellent at showing proof of activity and social validation.

What to avoid:

- The experience is highly transactional and can feel price-led.
- The density is useful, but the page becomes noisy quickly.
- The visual system is distinctive, but the brand energy is broad language-learning commerce, not high-trust academic support.

Takeaway for Mentor IB:

- Reuse the evidence density.
- Do not reuse the “endless commercial list of tutors” feeling.

### 3.2 Wyzant

What works:

- Immediate trust framing: review volume, scale, guarantees, university logos, press logos.
- Rich tutor rows are good for high-consideration comparison.
- Strong reassurance mechanics such as fit guarantees and no-upfront-fee framing.
- The experience tells users why they can trust the marketplace before asking them to choose.

What to avoid:

- The product feels broad, adult, and generalist.
- It is trustworthy, but not emotionally specific.
- The density can feel like research work rather than relief.

Takeaway for Mentor IB:

- Reuse the trust architecture.
- Avoid the “large national directory” tone.

### 3.3 GoStudent

What works:

- Positioning is clearly matching-first, not browsing-first.
- The language focuses on the right tutor, personalisation, learning plan, and confidence.
- The journey is guided in steps, which reduces choice overload.
- The design puts reassurance before exploration.

What to avoid:

- The brand is parent-led and broad-schooling oriented.
- The emotional register is generic “better grades and confidence”.
- The imagery and marketing tone are more K-12 than IB-specific.

Takeaway for Mentor IB:

- Reuse the matching model.
- Avoid generic school-success marketing.

### 3.4 Superprof

What works:

- Massive breadth and obvious social proof.
- Tutor cards quickly expose price and review signals.

What to avoid:

- The experience is over-broad and cluttered.
- It feels like a huge classified directory rather than a curated academic product.
- Subject sprawl weakens trust for specialist use cases.

Takeaway for Mentor IB:

- Broad supply is not the goal.
- Specialisation should feel like the advantage.

### 3.5 italki

What works:

- Friendly and approachable tone.
- Less intimidating than enterprise-style tutoring platforms.
- A more community-oriented, human feel than many directories.

What to avoid:

- The model is still broad-category browsing.
- It is not designed around high-stakes academic decision support.

Takeaway for Mentor IB:

- Human warmth matters.
- Warmth alone is not enough without expert-match clarity.

## 4. Competitive Pattern Synthesis

The strongest platforms do one of two things well:

- they build heavy trust and proof before asking users to browse
- they guide users into a match instead of exposing a cold directory first

The weakest experiences:

- feel like a giant list
- make every tutor look roughly interchangeable
- rely on generic cards and generic category taxonomies
- ask users to do too much evaluation work on their own

## 5. Strategic Recommendation

Mentor IB should reposition as:

**A guided IB tutor matching product for specific academic pressure points.**

Not:

- a general tutoring marketplace
- a neutral tutor directory
- a “find anyone for anything” search engine

### Core product promise

The platform should say, in both UX and visual language:

- tell us what kind of IB help you need
- we’ll narrow the field to the right kind of tutor
- we’ll explain why each match fits
- you can book confidently without doing hours of comparison work

### Recommended positioning pillars

- IB-native: built around IA, EE, TOK, IO, HL/SL, exam blocks, predicted grades, and subject-specific realities
- problem-first: starts from the student’s current need, not from an abstract tutor directory
- human matching: explains why a tutor is a fit, not just what tags they have
- calm authority: feels academically credible, not corporate, childish, or startup-generic

## 6. UX Strategy

### 6.1 Primary UX model

The default journey should be:

1. Identify the student’s situation
2. Narrow to a few strong tutor matches
3. Explain the reason for each match
4. Let the student compare or book

Search should still exist, but it should be secondary.

### 6.2 Information architecture shift

Current implied IA:

- Home
- Search
- Tutor profile
- Book

Recommended IA:

- Home
- Get matched
- Browse tutors
- Tutor profile
- Compare
- Book

### 6.3 The matching flow

The first-run flow should ask:

- What do you need help with right now?
- Which IB subject or component is involved?
- How urgent is it?
- What kind of help do you want?
- What language and timezone matter?

The first question should be problem-led, not subject-led.

Recommended starter options:

- IA feedback
- TOK essay or exhibition
- EE planning or review
- English IO or oral practice
- HL exam rescue
- Weekly support
- Predicted-grade improvement

### 6.4 Search page model

The current 3-column marketplace card logic should not define the new discovery page.

For high-consideration service selection, the main results layout should be a rich list or 2-column comparison layout, not a dense card grid.

Reason:

- students need more than face, price, and subject tags
- richer rows make space for teaching style, fit rationale, availability, and credibility
- list layouts are better for nuanced comparison than visual card walls

Recommended search structure:

- top bar with active problem context
- 4 or 5 most important filters exposed
- advanced filters in a drawer or side panel
- result list with “why this tutor fits” blocks
- save and compare for up to 3 tutors

### 6.5 Tutor card strategy

Each result should answer:

- what is this tutor best for?
- why are they a match for me?
- can I trust them?
- can I book them soon?

Recommended result card content:

- real photo
- name and short proof badge
- `Best for:` statement
- 2 or 3 match reasons
- rating and lesson proof
- language and timezone overlap
- next available slot or response speed
- price
- one main CTA
- save / compare

### 6.6 Tutor profile strategy

The profile should not start with biography. It should start with fit.

Recommended profile order:

- concise fit summary
- `Good fit if you need...`
- short teaching style summary
- recent student outcomes or review highlights
- expertise by IB use case
- availability and booking CTA
- detailed background and bio

The first screen should reduce uncertainty, not repeat metadata.

### 6.7 Comparison UX

Comparison is underused in tutoring products and is especially valuable here.

Mentor IB should support comparing saved tutors across:

- IB subjects and components
- teaching style
- languages
- time zone overlap
- availability
- price
- review strength
- best-for scenarios

This helps the product feel like a decision assistant instead of a directory.

## 7. Visual Direction

## Recommended Design Territory

**Editorial academic guidance**

This direction should feel:

- calm
- sharp
- trustworthy
- human
- specific
- non-generic

It should not feel:

- default SaaS
- glossy AI startup
- giant marketplace
- child-oriented edtech
- luxury consultant brand

### Visual references to borrow conceptually

- editorial rhythm rather than dashboard rhythm
- academic annotation rather than generic badges
- guided pathways rather than infinite browse walls
- warm, tactile surfaces rather than cold UI neutrals

### Core visual principles

- Fewer cards, more structure
- Stronger typography, fewer decorative effects
- Real human imagery only
- Problem-led sections and labels
- Proof and fit always visible
- Calm contrast, not bright-startup contrast

## 8. Design System Reset

This should be a genuine reset, not a tweak to the current system.

### 8.1 Typography

Avoid:

- Geist
- Inter
- Manrope
- the default geometric-sans stack that instantly reads as modern SaaS

Recommended type system:

- **Primary UI sans:** `IBM Plex Sans`, `Public Sans`, or `Suisse Int'l` if licensing is acceptable
- **Display / accent serif:** `Instrument Serif`, `Fraunces`, or `Tiempos` if licensing is acceptable
- **Utility mono:** `IBM Plex Mono` for schedules, timing, and data moments

Recommended pairing for implementation later:

- `IBM Plex Sans` + `Instrument Serif`

Why:

- it keeps the interface readable and operational
- it introduces an academic and editorial voice
- it moves the brand away from the “AI template” look

Use of serif should be controlled:

- hero emphasis
- section intros
- highlighted nouns or phrases
- not every heading

### 8.2 Color direction

Avoid:

- generic indigo-on-white startup palettes
- purple AI gradients
- cold grayscale interfaces

Recommended palette family:

- **Paper:** warm parchment / oat background
- **Ink:** deep charcoal for text
- **Primary:** deep bottle green or dark academic navy
- **Accent:** restrained coral or clay for calls to action and highlights
- **Support:** muted gold for merit / achievement accents only

Recommended emotional effect:

- feels like serious study support
- warmer and more memorable than fintech blue
- distinct from the default tutoring and SaaS palette set

### 8.3 Shape language

Avoid:

- all-purpose rounded-12 cards everywhere
- floating SaaS widgets as the default layout unit

Recommended:

- clearer section framing
- larger page-level containers
- mixed edge softness: mostly soft rectangles with occasional squared editorial dividers
- underlines, rules, tabs, and margin labels as part of hierarchy

### 8.4 Imagery policy

Do not mix:

- real photos
- AI portraits
- illustrations
- stock-style cutouts

Pick one system and enforce it.

Recommended:

- real tutor portraits
- real study-environment photography
- occasional abstract editorial texture

No synthetic-looking portraits in core trust surfaces.

### 8.5 Motion

Motion should support reassurance and orientation, not startup polish.

Recommended:

- gentle section reveals
- card emphasis on hover only where useful
- guided-step transitions
- anchored filter and compare interactions

Avoid:

- constant bounce
- exaggerated lift
- decorative parallax

## 9. UX Patterns To Reuse

- Guided intake before broad browsing
- Explicit match rationale on every result
- Guarantees and reassurance near the first CTA
- Rich tutor proof in results, not hidden behind profile clicks
- Free-trial or low-risk first step framing
- Compare flow for shortlisted tutors
- Strong applied-filter context and sorting transparency

## 10. UX Patterns To Avoid

- Generic hero plus generic “browse tutors” flow
- Tutor grids as the main comparison surface
- Overloaded horizontal filter bars as the only discovery control
- Startup-default blue gradients and geometric sans everywhere
- Trust hidden too low on the page
- Generic category systems that erase IB specificity
- Long bios before fit summaries
- Mixed-quality profile photos

## 11. Recommended Product Language

Move from:

- Find Tutor
- Browse tutors
- Book Lesson

Toward:

- Get matched
- Show me tutors for this
- Best fits for your IB situation
- Why this tutor fits
- Help with this part of IB

This is a small copy change with big positioning impact.

## 12. Product-Level Recommendation

Yes, a fresh start is justified on the design side.

The reason is that a generic marketplace interaction model, visual language, and emotional framing are optimized for the wrong category.

The right move is:

- keep the useful product learnings
- reset the design system
- reset the information architecture
- reset the discovery model around matching
- rebuild the front-end around a stronger UX thesis

4. Low-fidelity wireframes for Home, Match Flow, Search Results, Tutor Profile, Compare
